For international visitors

Your complete guide to visiting Andaman from abroad

Visas, permits, flights, money, and everything else you'll want to sort before you land — written by someone who answers these questions every week.

Every week we get emails from travellers in Germany, Australia, the US, Singapore — all asking some version of the same question: "Can I actually visit the Andaman Islands as a foreigner, and what do I need to sort out first?" The short answer is yes, most nationalities can. The longer answer is what this guide is for.

The Andamans are still relatively uncrowded compared to Thailand or Bali — you won't be fighting for a sunlounger at Radhanagar, at least not at 7am. But getting here takes a little more planning if you're coming from outside India. Here's everything, in order.

Step one: your India visa

You need a valid Indian visa to enter the Andamans — the islands are part of India, not a separate jurisdiction. Citizens of over 160 countries can apply for an Indian e-Visa online at the official Government of India portal. The Tourist e-Visa (30-day single-entry or 1-year multiple-entry) covers leisure travel to the Andamans. Apply at least 4 business days before travel; most approvals come through within 72 hours.

Nationalities that cannot use e-Visa

Citizens of Pakistan, Afghanistan, and a small number of other countries cannot use the e-Visa route and must apply through an Indian embassy or consulate. If you're unsure, check the official IVFRT portal or contact the nearest Indian diplomatic mission.

Visa on arrival

India's Visa on Arrival scheme (a separate, stricter channel from e-Visa) is available to citizens of Japan, South Korea, UAE, and a few other specific nationalities at designated airports. Port Blair airport is not one of the designated VoA points — so you'll need to sort your visa before flying to the Andamans, regardless of which route you take.

The Restricted Area Permit — what it is and why it matters

Here's the one thing that catches international travellers off guard: the Andaman Islands are a Protected Area under Indian law, which means foreign nationals need a Restricted Area Permit (RAP) in addition to their visa. Don't let this worry you — it's free and issued on arrival at Port Blair airport or the harbour. You just fill out a form and hand it over with your passport. The whole thing takes under five minutes.

What the RAP covers

The standard RAP allows you to visit Port Blair, Havelock (Swaraj Dweep), Neil Island (Shaheed Dweep), Baratang, North Bay, Ross Island, Cinque Island, and most of the areas you'd want to visit anyway. It's valid for 30 days and issued at the first port of entry.

Islands that require extra permits

A handful of areas need a separate Tribal Area Permit on top of the RAP — specifically the Nicobar Islands and any island with protected indigenous communities. Most international tourists never need these. If you're planning to visit Diglipur or Little Andaman, speak with us first — the rules there are more nuanced.

Getting here: your flight options

There's no direct international service into Port Blair (Veer Savarkar International Airport, IXZ). You'll connect via one of three main Indian hubs.

Via Chennai (Madras)

The most common route. Chennai connects to Port Blair 6–8 times daily across IndiGo, Air India, SpiceJet, and Go First. Flight time is about 2 hours. Chennai airport (MAA) has decent international connections from Singapore, Kuala Lumpur, Dubai, Colombo, and a number of Southeast Asian cities. If you're coming from Singapore, a direct SQ/MH flight to Chennai + same-day onward to Port Blair is genuinely convenient.

Via Kolkata (Calcutta)

Similar flight time to Port Blair — around 2 hours. Kolkata has international routes from Bangkok, Dubai, Dhaka, Kathmandu, and Singapore. Slightly fewer daily services to Port Blair than Chennai, so check timing carefully if you're on a tight schedule.

Via Delhi (Indira Gandhi)

Delhi works best if you're entering India from Europe, the US, or Central Asia. IGI airport is one of India's biggest hubs. The catch: Delhi to Port Blair is 3.5–4 hours, which adds a lot to your day. A night in Delhi or Kolkata before heading south is often worth it.

Booking tip

Book your international-to-India leg and your India-to-Port Blair leg as separate tickets — you'll almost always pay less, and it gives you flexibility if there's a delay. Allow a minimum of 3 hours for the domestic connection; customs, immigration, and the domestic terminal transfer can eat time.

Money, ATMs, and what things cost

India runs on the Indian Rupee (INR). At time of writing, rough conversions: USD 1 ≈ ₹84, EUR 1 ≈ ₹91, GBP 1 ≈ ₹107, SGD 1 ≈ ₹63. Check live rates the week you travel.

ATMs in Port Blair

SBI, HDFC, and ICICI ATMs are easy to find in Aberdeen Bazaar and near Phoenix Bay. They accept Visa and Mastercard. Daily withdrawal limits are typically ₹10,000–20,000 per transaction. Bring a card that doesn't charge international fees — cards like Wise, Revolut, and Charles Schwab (US) work well here.

ATMs on Havelock and Neil

There's one SBI ATM near Havelock jetty and one on Neil Island. They run out of cash on weekends. Carry enough notes before you cross — ideally ₹5,000–10,000 in cash when leaving Port Blair.

What to budget

Budget travellers can get by on ₹2,500–3,500/day covering guesthouse, local food, and ferry transport. Mid-range (comfortable resort, restaurant meals, one or two activities) runs ₹6,000–10,000/day per person. The dive operators and better beach resorts on Havelock are the biggest cost drivers — a Discover Scuba session is around ₹4,500–5,500 per person.

Getting between islands

Inter-island travel is almost entirely by ferry, and this is where international travellers often run into trouble — not because it's difficult, but because ferries are popular and sell out.

Government ferries

The Makruzz, Nautika, and Coastal Cruise ferries connect Port Blair to Havelock and Neil daily, departing Phoenix Bay Jetty at 6:00–6:30am and a midday sailing. Journey time is about 90 minutes to Havelock. Book through the official Andaman Nicobar Ferries website or through us — don't rely on buying tickets on the day during November through February.

Private speedboats

Private charter boats are available for the Port Blair–Havelock run (approximately ₹12,000–18,000 per boat) and are worth it if you have a group of 4+ or need flexibility on timing. They're faster (45–60 minutes) and skip the queue entirely.

Local transport on the islands

Auto-rickshaws dominate Port Blair. On Havelock and Neil, you'll hire mopeds (₹400–500/day) or book local autos. No Uber, no Ola — it's cash in an auto or hire a bike. Most roads are in reasonable condition; the Havelock main road is fully paved now.

SIM cards and connectivity

Foreign SIM cards don't roam well here — even Indian SIMs from Delhi or Mumbai sometimes struggle. The recommended approach: get a local BSNL prepaid SIM from the BSNL office near the Aberdeen Bazaar in Port Blair (bring your passport for KYC). BSNL is the only carrier with reliable 4G coverage across the smaller islands. Jio and Airtel work in Port Blair but get patchy on Havelock and almost nonexistent on Neil. Expect to pay around ₹200–300 for a week of data.

Wi-Fi situation

Most resorts on Havelock and Neil offer Wi-Fi, but it's slow and unreliable outside peak hours. If you need to work remotely, Port Blair is your best bet — there are proper cafés with decent broadband near Aberdeen Bazaar. The islands are genuinely a place to disconnect, not work.

Best time to visit

October to May is the window you want. November through February is peak season — dry, clear skies, calm seas, excellent visibility underwater. March and April stay dry but get hot (34–36°C). Early October and late May are shoulder months — fewer crowds, slightly choppy seas on some days, but still mostly beautiful.

Avoid June through September. The southwest monsoon hits the Andamans hard — frequent closures of beaches and dive sites, ferries cancelled without notice, and half the accommodation shuttered. Not worth it unless you specifically want the dramatic skies and empty beaches, and even then the ferry disruptions make island-hopping genuinely difficult.

Health, safety, and practical notes

Water

Don't drink tap water. Bottled water (Bisleri 1L is ₹20 everywhere) is the standard. Most restaurants use filtered water for cooking but ask if you're unsure. A personal filter bottle (like a LifeStraw) is useful if you're going off-grid in Diglipur or Baratang.

Medical

G.B. Pant Hospital in Port Blair handles emergencies and has decent basic facilities. Havelock has a small government clinic but for anything serious you'd be medevaced back to Port Blair or further. Travel insurance with medical evacuation cover is not optional here — it's load-bearing. Get it.

Sun and heat

Reef-safe sunscreen (mineral-based, no oxybenzone or octinoxate) is not just the environmental choice — some dive operators now require it on their boats. Bring your own from home; quality reef-safe options are hard to source in Port Blair.

Tipping

Not obligatory in the way it is in the US, but warmly received. ₹50–100 for a restaurant meal, ₹200–500 for a full-day guide or dive instructor, ₹100 for an auto driver who waited while you swam. Cash tips directly to your guide beat everything else.

A sample 7-day itinerary for international visitors

Day 1 — Port Blair: Arrive, collect your RAP at the airport, settle in. Evening walk through Aberdeen Bazaar, dinner at New Lighthouse restaurant on Marine Hill (the fish curry at table by the window, overlooking the harbour).

Day 2 — Port Blair: Cellular Jail tour at 9am — the light-and-sound show that evening runs in English on Fridays. North Bay Island scuba intro or sea walk in the afternoon.

Day 3 — Baratang: Mangrove creek to limestone caves and mud volcanoes. The boat through the narrow creek at sunrise is worth the 5am start. Back in Port Blair by 4pm.

Day 4 — Havelock: Morning ferry at 6:15am. Check in, rent a moped, Radhanagar Beach by 3pm. Stay for the light shift around 5pm when the day-trippers leave.

Day 5 — Havelock: Full-day dive at Dixon's Pinnacle or Aquarium (certified) or Discover Scuba at Elephant Beach (beginner). Bioluminescent kayaking after dark — book through your resort the night before.

Day 6 — Neil Island: Short ferry from Havelock (45 min). Rent a cycle (₹100/day), ride to Natural Bridge and Laxmanpur Beach. Quieter than Havelock, better for sunset watching.

Day 7 — Port Blair: Return ferry, afternoon at your leisure, evening flight out.

One thing people always ask

Is the Andaman worth the extra effort compared to, say, Thailand or the Maldives? Yes — but for different reasons. It's not a party scene and it doesn't have the polished resort infrastructure of Phuket. What it has is genuinely good reefs (healthier than much of Thailand's), near-empty beaches between November and January, and a pace that feels like somewhere that hasn't been completely packaged yet. If you want cocktails at sunset on a sunlounger, Thailand does that better. If you want to feel like you found something most people haven't — the Andamans win.
